Tendinitis (Tendonitis) First Aid

This page provides information about tendinitis, its causes, symptoms, and treatment options. Remember, this information is for general knowledge and does not substitute professional medical advice. If you suspect you have tendinitis, please consult a healthcare professional for proper diagnosis and a personalized treatment plan.

Understanding Tendinitis

Tendinitis is the inflammation or irritation of a tendon, the thick fibrous cords that attach muscle to bone. This inflammation can cause pain and tenderness just outside a joint. Common sites include the shoulders, elbows, wrists, knees, and ankles.

Causes of Tendinitis

Tendinitis is often the result of repetitive motions or overuse that strain the tendons. Other contributing factors include:

  • Repetitive movements, especially with poor technique.
  • Sudden increase in activity level.
  • Improper technique during sports or other activities.
  • Age-related loss of tendon flexibility.
  • Underlying medical conditions like diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, and gout.
  • Direct injury or trauma to a tendon.
  • Poor conditioning and weak surrounding muscles.
  • Lack of proper warm-up before activity.

Symptoms of Tendinitis

Symptoms of tendinitis can vary depending on the affected tendon but commonly include:

  • Pain at the site of the affected tendon, often a dull ache that worsens with movement.
  • Tenderness to the touch along the tendon.
  • Stiffness in the joint, especially in the morning or after rest.
  • Pain that worsens during or after activity.
  • Mild swelling in the affected area.
  • A grating or crackling sensation (crepitus) when moving the tendon (in some cases).
  • Weakness in the affected area.

Cure/Treatment of Tendinitis

The primary goals of treatment are to relieve pain and inflammation, promote healing of the tendon, and restore full function.

Immediate Self-Care (R.I.C.E.)

  • Rest: Avoid activities that aggravate the pain. Modify your work or sports routine as needed.
  • Ice: Apply ice packs wrapped in a cloth for 15-20 minutes several times a day to reduce pain and swelling.
  • Compression: Wrap the affected area with an elastic bandage to help reduce swelling. Ensure it's not too tight.
  • Elevation: Keep the injured area raised above the level of your heart to help reduce swelling.
  • Pain Relief: Over-the-counter medications like NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) or acetaminophen can help manage pain and inflammation. Topical pain relievers may also provide some relief.

Later Stage Treatment and Rehabilitation

  • Rest and Activity Modification: Continue to avoid pain-causing activities. Gradually reintroduce activities with proper technique.
  • Physical Therapy: A tailored exercise program to improve flexibility, strength, and range of motion. Includes stretching, strengthening, and biomechanics correction.
  • Stretching: Gentle exercises to maintain or improve flexibility and reduce stiffness.
  • Strengthening Exercises: Exercises to support the affected tendon by strengthening surrounding muscles.
  • Bracing or Splinting: May be used for immobilization to allow the tendon to rest and heal.
  • Corticosteroid Injections: Can provide short-term pain and inflammation relief but repeated use can weaken the tendon.
  • Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) Injections: May be considered in some cases to promote healing.
  • Surgery: Usually a last resort for severe or chronic tendinitis when other treatments fail.

Important Considerations

  • Early Intervention: Addressing tendinitis early improves recovery chances.
  • Proper Diagnosis: Crucial to rule out other conditions.
  • Follow Rehabilitation Program: Essential for full recovery and preventing recurrence.
  • Address Underlying Causes: Identify and correct factors like poor technique or equipment.
  • Gradual Return to Activity: Increase activity levels slowly as pain allows.
  • Prevention: Proper warm-up, technique, conditioning, and avoiding overuse are key.

Healing time for tendinitis varies. Mild cases may improve in weeks, while chronic or severe cases can take months of consistent treatment and rehabilitation. Patience and adherence to your healthcare provider's recommendations are vital.
