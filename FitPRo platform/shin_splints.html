Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome - MTSS)

This page provides information about shin splints, a common condition among athletes. Remember, this information is for general knowledge and does not substitute professional medical advice. If you are experiencing persistent or severe shin pain, consult a healthcare professional for proper diagnosis and management, especially to rule out other conditions like stress fractures.

Understanding Shin Splints

Shin splints refer to pain along the inner border of the tibia (shin bone). It's a common condition among athletes, especially runners, and is often considered an overuse injury. While not a strain or sprain in the traditional sense, it involves inflammation and micro-tears in the muscles, tendons, and bone tissue around the tibia.

Causes of Shin Splints

Shin splints typically develop gradually due to repetitive stress on the shinbone and the connective tissues that attach the muscles to the bone. Common contributing factors include:

  • Overpronation: Excessive inward rolling of the foot during walking or running. This puts extra stress on the muscles and tendons in the lower leg.
  • Sudden increase in training intensity or volume: Doing too much too soon without allowing the body to adapt.
  • Running on hard surfaces: Increases the impact on the shins.
  • Wearing improper or worn-out footwear: Lack of support and cushioning.
  • Weak or inflexible calf muscles: These muscles help stabilize the lower leg, and weakness or tightness can overload other tissues.
  • Running uphill or downhill: These activities place additional stress on the shin muscles.
  • Biomechanical issues: Leg length discrepancies, flat feet, or high arches can contribute.

Symptoms of Shin Splints

The pain associated with shin splints usually develops gradually and can range from mild to severe. Common symptoms include:

  • Pain along the inner border of the tibia (shin bone): This is the hallmark symptom. The pain can be sharp, throbbing, or dull.
  • Tenderness to the touch along the shin bone.
  • Pain that worsens during or after exercise.
  • Pain that may initially subside with rest but returns with activity.
  • Swelling in the lower leg (less common but can occur).
  • Numbness or weakness in the feet (rare).

Cure/Treatment of Shin Splints

The primary goals of treatment are to reduce pain and inflammation, allow the tissues to heal, and address the underlying causes to prevent recurrence.

Immediate Self-Care (R.I.C.E. and Modifications)

  • Rest: Reduce or stop activities that aggravate the pain. This doesn't necessarily mean complete inactivity, but rather modifying your routine to avoid high-impact exercises. Cross-training activities like swimming or cycling may be possible if they don't cause pain.
  • Ice: Apply ice packs wrapped in a cloth to the affected area for 15-20 minutes several times a day to reduce pain and inflammation.
  • Compression: Wrapping the lower leg with an elastic bandage can help reduce swelling.
  • Elevation: Elevating the leg when resting can also help reduce swelling.
  • Pain Relief: Over-the-counter pain relievers like NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) or acetaminophen can help manage pain and inflammation.

Later Stage Treatment and Rehabilitation

  • Activity Modification: Gradually reintroduce activities as pain subsides, starting with low-impact options and slowly increasing intensity and duration. Avoid activities that consistently trigger the pain.
  • Stretching: Gentle stretching of the calf muscles (gastrocnemius and soleus) and the muscles in the front of the shin can help improve flexibility and reduce tension.
  • Strengthening Exercises: Strengthening the calf muscles, as well as the muscles in the front of the shin (tibialis anterior), and the muscles of the foot and ankle can improve stability and support.
  • Proper Footwear: Ensure you are wearing supportive athletic shoes that are appropriate for your activity and foot type. Replace worn-out shoes. Consider orthotic inserts if recommended by a healthcare professional, especially if you have overpronation or other biomechanical issues.
  • Physical Therapy: A physical therapist can provide a tailored exercise program, manual therapy techniques, and guidance on proper biomechanics and training progression.
  • Addressing Biomechanical Issues: If overpronation or other biomechanical factors are contributing, orthotic inserts or changes in running technique may be recommended.
  • Gradual Return to Activity: Increase training intensity and volume gradually, following the "10% rule" (increasing mileage or intensity by no more than 10% per week).

Important Considerations

  • Early Intervention: Addressing shin splints early can prevent them from becoming chronic and more severe.
  • Proper Diagnosis: While self-care can be helpful for mild cases, persistent or severe pain warrants a medical evaluation to rule out other conditions like stress fractures.
  • Prevention: Focus on proper training progression, appropriate footwear, stretching and strengthening exercises, and addressing any biomechanical issues.
  • Listen to Your Body: Pay attention to pain signals and back off if the pain returns or worsens.

Recovery time varies depending on the severity of the shin splints and how well the treatment plan is followed. Mild cases may improve within a few weeks, while more severe cases can take several months. The principles of rest, ice, and gradual return to activity are still important aspects of recovery.
